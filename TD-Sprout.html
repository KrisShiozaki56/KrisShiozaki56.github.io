TD Lab Sprout

Initiating and designing Sprout, TD Lab's first design system.





Overview
A Design System currently consisting of a Sketch component library and usage guidelines, to be used by TD Lab to increase the speed of design and develop and decision-making for Lab projects.


Team
I was the lead designer and worked with two other designers at the Lab.


Contributions
I took ownership of research, content writing, design and the planning that went into this project.


Timeline
2 weeks in August 2020


Tools
Figma, Sketch, Confluence, CSS

Project Background

Sprout> offers visual style and accessible UI components, released as a CSS and Flutter framework and Sketch components library and documented on Confluence. It was produced by a team of 3 designers in order to serve 20+ POC mobile and web application products and experiences.





Problem
The majority of projects at the Lab are proof of concepts (POCs), where each one consists of a few sprints completed over the course of 3 to 5 weeks, making time a scarce resource. Once we reach the design and development stages of the project, we found ourselves recreating widely-used components from scratch (i.e. buttons, tabs) every time. And sometimes for new designers that join the team every 4 months (co-op students), they spend too much time thinking of and seeking past designs for reference for their designs.

Additionally, there was sometimes confusion around which components were most appropriate to use (i.e. tabs versus accordions) and we usually referred to examples in previous projects to re-learn the contexts of component usages.


Starting from scratch to design and develop components for every project and determine each components usage, is time consuming.




Goal
To provide designers and developers with component templates and guidelines to streamline project design and development processes.

Central KPI
Productivity - the systems team improves average velocity

Additional Metrics
Time spent designing high fidelity mockups, time spent developing the front-end of individual screens

Primary Research

Key Findings
I interviewed 5 full-time and co-op Lab employees to further understand the value a design system would have on its users and to gain more insight into the Lab's workflow.


Secondary Research

Competitive Analysis
Some interviewees had mentioned a few Design Systems that the Lab has referred to when completing various projects in the past. I set out to explore the few mentioend, which include: Human Interface Design Guidelines, Material Design Guidelines and Shopify Polaris. Doing so will help me better understand how designs systems are structured in addition to the level of detail component documentation should be, what communication styles to use and more.



Takeaways
1. Opinionated Documentation
Polaris' component usage guidelines focus on how Shopify uses the components versus how the components are generally used (i.e. specific Shopify use cases for a button). Firstly, TD Lab is a very small team and documentation should be specific to their types of projects - otherwise, we can continue to read into the three systems listed above. We must stick to the idea that all design system documentation should be tailored to the Lab.

2. Visuals and Interactive Examples
Material Design and Polaris both showcase both static visuals and interactive examples for components to showcase how the different states link together. Interactive examples will be out of the preliminary scope due to time constraints. However, if we consider that we will prioritize documentation for mobile usage, static visuals of the components and their anatomy and specifications will suffice.


Planning

It was decided that we would start with building out a Sketch Component library but before we begin any tasks, we needed to have a plan and prioritize the inclusion of components based on their common usage and the time it takes to design and document them.

With the other two designers, I led a brainstorming session where we revisited past projects, compiled a list of components, and then on a 2 x 2 matrix, laid out which items to tackle first after considering the concerns outlined above.



Design, Develop, Document


This section will outline the general process taken to build out the components of the Design System, accompanied by examples of this process applied to Buttons

Design
This did not always mean building from scratch - in fact that was rarely the case. Our team compiled an inventory of UI components from past projects, where adjustments concerned styling and/or the further design of component states.

Additionally, we also considered accessibility by ensuring colour contrasts met the standards outlined by AODA.




Develop
Due to insufficient resources, we were unable to find developers for the 2 weeks I was on the project. But I consulted one of the lead developers of the Lab to see what specifications they may require in terms of styling of the component (colours, padding, etc.). While they mentioned that the team primarily uses Flutter, I asked if documenting styling in CSS could be helpful. When we consider web in the future, that may be the case but even for now, they were happy with anything we could provide as CSS could be translated into the language they prefer to document in, in the future.

Hence during this term, I outlined basic styling such as padding within a component and typography considerations.




Document
Once the component designs and developed styling had been finalized, we held design reviews and discussed the most common use cases of the component in talks, at TD Lab. We considered both general usage within an application as well as its specific uses for TD Lab.
Documentation included principles, component states (if applicable), specifications, and Rules.

Next Steps

Starter UI Kit
While the Sketch Component library of Sprout is set up with the new colour and text styles and master symbols, the next step is to provide a page with the most common components and example mockups for anyone to use as a reference.

Finalization of Component Designs
And even though development had not officially started, there is much left for design to cover before they do. Further iterations for component adjustments need to be made to ensure dimensions are reasonable and that colours meet accessibility requirements. Additionally, documentation will need to be further flushed out and the new team should ensure the documentation zeroes in on the usages of components at TD Lab, which also considering the Lab's principles and values.

Publish
Currently, Sprout lives on Confluence and migrating to a separate platform may be ideal in the future, especially once development documentation commences. Confluence pages are not dynamic and do not support interaction between users and the page and so interactive examples of components would remain out of scope. However, Confluence is a good starting point for the documentation where everything can live in one place.
