Project Voice

Helping you execute your best presentations!

Overview
Project Voice is an Android application designed to assist individuals of all ages in preparing for presentations by recording & outputing a variety of metrics through voice analysis.

Check out our Invision prototype, which will walk you through the app! Development is in progress.



Team Size
6 [1 PM, 4 Devs, 1 Design]


Role
Designer


Timeline
February 2019 - May 2019


Tools

BACKGROUND

In the first 4 months of 2019, I had the privilege to co-op at Connected, a software product development firm based in downtown Toronto. Check out the co-ops' Medium blog post to learn about our time there!

The first step was to determine what everyone wanted to learn from this experience. Each of the co-ops came from different undergraduate majors where 4 were from computer science, 1 from ECE & 1 from SYDE. We first decided on a tech stack - Android Studio, to create an Android application. Next came the division of roles, where the majority of us would be developers. For instance, one had interest in learning how to organize a project and would become our PM.

My interests in learning more about official design processes led me to become the lead designer. However, everyone would be free to work with someone in a different role (e.g. developers can design or assist the PM).

In Startup culture, there are many opportunities for people to present and hold company-wide discussions/meetings. Connected embraces this culture and the addition of being a product development firm became the source of inspiration for our idea to make a presentation aid.


Problem
It takes a very long time to identify technical oral errors while practicing for a presentation (e.g. videotaping a runthrough & rewatching is heavily reliant on your own ability to find errors)


Solution
A product to assist in preparing for presentations that will reduce 'review time' after each runthrough.


We gave the project the codename 'Project Voice' and are continuing to work on it outside of company time. This page will summarize my contributions, findings & learnings from the design process.

BRAINSTORMING

With only 4 months, we determined that the best course of action would be to design in parallel with development. I decided to start by drawing 2 basic user journeys - one covering the installation of the app while the other primarily covered app functionality.

We set 3 main goals, which would outline the purpose of the product. Since we would be working on this project outside of company time and many of us had other commitments, we decided that these goals would act as functionality benchmarks.


GOALS

  • Reduce the review time after a presentation / pitch runthrough
  • Use at least 3 metrics to allow users to determine how they feel they did from a technical standpoint
  • Allow users to save & review past recordings

Shortly after, we drafted some task flows & determined how many screens would be needed & which ones to prioritize. Little did we know that we were completely off the mark at the time!

PLANNING & WIREFRAMES

Shortly after drafting our ideas, I scheduled a meeting with a product designer at Connected and she gave us a lesson on breaking down task flows with edge cases, creating wireframes & user flows. After, I created a basic wireflow diagram and 2 primary task flows: new recording & viewing/editing past recordings.

Iterations Upon Iterations

Not everything works out the first time and Project Voice was no different. Each final screen was not achieved on the first try and many designs were created leading up to them. Although the application is not very complex with only 2 major task flows, the designs needed to be simple in a way that would not confuse the end users, which meant constantly trying new designs and asking for feedback.

1. Recording Screens
Another tough design decision was determining how to display past recording history & new recording functions. I tried a variety of layouts including a swipe-up option to reveal past recordings, tabs and a page displaying new & past recordings.


The layout was important because we wanted to limit the cognitive load as much as possible, which ended up eliminating the third option. The reason being that it displayed numerous components at once, which were not all needed for the user to perform one simple task. The swipe-up option seemed a bit difficult to understand at a first glance, which then eliminated that screen. The tabs came with many pros and was later deemed the most suitable layout for our app.

2. Pause Screen
The pause screen was one of the tougher UI design choices because we found it difficult to communicate that the user has the option to pause & unpause in addition to being able to save or discard the recording.


While it may seem small, the size of the buttons, words vs. icons, colours - all of these play a part in reducing confusion for the user. I had to ask many questions:

  • Should the icons be filled or outlined?
  • Do the users need buttons with words?
  • Should I pair icons with words on buttons?

I asked numerous people with little to no background about the application we were building, to briefly test a few screens. I created multiple 3-screen prototypes and conducted A/B testing, where one screen would be different and watched to see how they reacted when they saw the different screens. In addition to my observations, I briefly asked for their opinions after. These mini test sessions with potential users helped our team in making these sorts of design decisions.

User Flows

I created a high-fidelity user flow diagram using the final mockups, which captures the two major task flows outlined earlier. The purpose of this diagram is to guide the developers to ensure the UX is implemented as planned. It also helps everyone to understand the user's journey in our app & allows us to identify edge cases.

The next steps for the user flow diagram will be to add brief notes regarding animations & transitions underneath the mockups, as well as include screens, which tackle edge cases.

User Testing Plan

I worked with a UX design researcher at Connected, where she helped me create a user testing plan. Unfortunately, the plan itself cannot be shared due to branding regulations, but the plan outline included the following:

  • Objective of Test Session
  • Summary of Interview Plan (list)
  • Introduction (briefly introduce ourselves & the project)
  • Exploring Problem Space (gain an understanding of the users' related experience to the product)
  • Usability Test Cases Outline (Brief description & goals for each test)
  • Setting the Stage (Have the user imagine different scenarios)
  • 2 Test cases (Goal, Task, Observations, Discussion questions)
  • Conclusion (thank you & final questions)

Unfortunately, my co-op term ended before I was able to have some user testing sessions with my co-workers but now if any members of our team would like to conduct a user testing session, the documentation is there!

Reflection

This project was many of my firsts - my first time taking on a design role in a long-term team project, my first time creating user flows, my first co-op term, etc. It was a wonderful learning experience and I was able to take away invaluable skills. The next steps for this project include completing the development of the application, implementing more creative features & metrics into the application & conducting user testing. I hope to one day hold a user testing session!
